How to Remember Organic Chemistry Reactions for CEE

Organic Chemistry confused me for the first two months of CEE preparation. The moment I stopped treating reactions as a list to memorize and started connecting them as a map, everything changed.

By Ramu Pandey Â· Published: March 21, 2026 Â· 7 min read

Organic Chemistry looks enormous when you approach it chapter by chapter. Alkanes, alkenes, alkynes, alcohols, aldehydes, ketones each with their own set of reactions and reagents. I spent weeks reading and re-reading and still blanked on product-prediction questions. The issue was not effort. The issue was method.

What finally worked was treating all the reactions as a connected map rather than a list of isolated facts. Once I could see how one functional group converted to another, the logic became clear and the recall became automatic.

Build a reaction map on one page

This is the most practical thing I did for Organic Chemistry. I drew a single A4-size map with functional groups as nodes and arrows showing conversions. Each arrow had two things written beside it: the reagent and the type of reaction (oxidation, reduction, addition, substitution, elimination).

Exception sheet: I kept a second page specifically for exceptions and traps reactions that behave differently from the pattern. CEE often tests these. Things like Markovnikov exceptions with peroxides, or primary vs secondary alcohol oxidation products. This page saved me several marks.

Active recall practice, the part most students skip

After building the map, I practiced recall in two directions: cover the product and recall the reagent, then cover the reagent and recall the product. Both directions matter because CEE questions come from both angles.

Every two days I picked 15 random reactions from the map and wrote them out from memory. I checked mistakes immediately and updated the exception sheet if needed. This 15-minute daily session replaced two hours of passive reading and gave far better results.

Common questions I had and the answers

Do I need to understand mechanisms for CEE?
Basic mechanism logic is enough. You do not need arrow-pushing detail. Understanding whether a reaction is SN1, SN2, or electrophilic addition helps you predict products and avoid trap options.

What should I revise in the final week?
Only your reaction map, your exception sheet, and your personal error log. No new chapters.

Why do I keep losing easy marks in organic?
Usually because you forgot the reagent conditions, not the reaction itself. Add reagent conditions to your map explicitly not just "oxidizing agent" but specifically KMnOâ‚„ or PCC.
