How to Score Full Marks in CEE MAT Section

MAT is 20 marks in CEE and most students treat it as an afterthought. I used to do the same. Then I realized it was the easiest section to improve and I started preparing it daily from the beginning.

By Ramu Pandey · Published: March 21, 2026 · 7 min read

The Mental Agility Test section carries 20 marks in CEE, the same as an entire subject. But because it is not part of the Biology/Chemistry/Physics syllabus, most students push it to the last few weeks and end up underprepared. I made this mistake too in the first month of preparation.

What changed my approach was realizing that MAT is the section where consistent short practice gives the fastest improvement. You do not need to understand complex theory. You need to recognize patterns and build speed. Both of these come with repetition, not with intensive study sessions.

What MAT tests in CEE

SubsectionQuestionsWhat It Tests
Verbal Reasoning5Analogies, odd-one-out, syllogisms, word relationships
Numerical Reasoning5Number series, arithmetic patterns, percentage and ratio
Logical Sequencing5Direction sense, blood relations, coding-decoding
Spatial / Abstract Reasoning5Mirror images, rotations, paper folding, pattern matrices

Each subsection has 5 questions. That predictability is useful , you can structure your daily practice around exactly these four types and track your accuracy in each one separately.

My 30-minute daily MAT routine

I kept MAT practice short and daily rather than long and irregular. 30 minutes every morning, before the main study session. This built both speed and consistency without taking significant time away from Biology, Chemistry, and Physics.

TimeTask
0–8 minVerbal reasoning → analogies and odd-one-out
8–16 minNumerical reasoning → number series and arithmetic
16–23 minLogical sequencing → direction and coding problems
23–30 minAbstract reasoning → figure patterns and rotations

The time limits per subsection are deliberate. MAT rewards controlled speed, not rushing. If I could not solve a question within about 45 seconds, I marked it and moved on. Returning with fresh eyes in the last few minutes works better than stalling on one puzzle.

The pattern recognition effect: After three to four weeks of daily MAT practice, question types start feeling familiar even when the specific content changes. This is the real benefit , not that you have seen the answer before, but that you recognize the question structure and know where to look first.

Common mistakes I made early on

Rushing without reading carefully. MAT questions often use words like "not," "except," "least," or "only" that completely change the answer. I missed several questions early on simply because I read too fast and missed the key qualifier. Slowing down for 3 seconds on each question costs less than the marks you lose from misreading.

Spending too long on abstract reasoning. Figure-based questions can feel time-consuming. If a pattern is not clear within 30 seconds, use elimination : rule out options that are obviously wrong and move on. Stalling costs more marks than a considered elimination guess.

Skipping MAT entirely during Biology-heavy study weeks. Even 15 minutes of MAT practice is enough to maintain the pattern recognition you have built. Dropping it entirely for a week means the patterns feel less automatic, and speed drops.

Exam-day strategy for MAT

I attempted MAT early in the exam , usually in the first 30 minutes because the questions are typically faster to solve than Physics numericals or Biology diagram questions. Building momentum with MAT before the heavier sections helped me settle into the exam rhythm.

  • Start with whichever MAT subsection you are strongest at to build confidence
  • Set a personal 45-second limit per question , mark anything that exceeds it
  • Use elimination on abstract reasoning questions rather than attempting to solve every detail
  • Return to marked MAT questions after completing the other sections if time allows
