Chemistry Reaction Maps for Faster Recall

Instead of memorizing reactions in isolation, I built one map that linked every common conversion. That single page cut my confusion in half and made product-prediction questions feel obvious.

By Ramu Pandey Â· Published: March 21, 2026 Â· 6 min read

Organic Chemistry used to be my least confident section. Not because I had not studied it, I had read the chapters multiple times but because every reaction felt like a separate isolated fact. When a CEE question asked what product forms when a primary alcohol is treated with a mild oxidizing agent, I would hesitate even though I had read the answer recently. The connection between "primary alcohol" and "aldehyde via mild oxidation" was not automatic enough.

The moment I stopped treating reactions as a list and started drawing them as a connected map, that hesitation went away. The map made the connections visible, and visible connections are far easier to recall under time pressure than lists of facts.

What a reaction map looks like

A reaction map is a single page where functional groups are nodes and arrows show the conversions between them. Each arrow has two pieces of information: the reagent and the type of reaction. That is all it needs.

The two pages I kept

Page 1 â†’ The reaction map: Full conversion chain for all main functional groups. I redrew this from memory every two days. If I could not reproduce an arrow, I had identified a gap.

Page 2 â†’ The exception sheet: Reactions that break the general pattern. Markovnikov exceptions with peroxides. The fact that secondary alcohols oxidize to ketones, not aldehydes. The conditions where dehydration gives an alkene versus an ether. CEE regularly tests these edge cases because they are exactly the things students get wrong.

I reviewed both pages before every Chemistry test. Together they took about 8 minutes and covered the most likely traps.

How to use the map for active recall: Cover the product side and try to recall it from reagent and starting material. Then flip and cover the reagent and try to recall it from the starting material and product. Both directions appear in CEE questions.
