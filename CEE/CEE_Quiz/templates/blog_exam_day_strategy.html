CEE Exam Day Strategy: Do's and Don'ts

Months of preparation can unravel in 180 minutes if you go in without a clear plan. Here is the exam-day system I built and the mistakes I learned to avoid.

By Ramu Pandey · Published: March 21, 2026 · 8 min read

The night before CEE, I made the mistake of trying to revise one more chapter. I stayed up later than I should have, felt anxious, and walked into the exam hall the next morning already tired. The exam itself went worse than my mock tests not because I had studied less, but because I went in without a settled plan for how to spend 180 minutes.

What I know now is that exam day performance is a skill separate from preparation. You can know your content well and still lose marks through poor time allocation, panic decisions, and bad negative marking judgment. This guide is the system I wish I had followed.

The night before: do less, not more

The evening before CEE is not revision time. Your brain needs consolidation, not new input. The most useful thing you can do the night before is a light review of your formula list, reaction map, and error log things you already know, not things you are trying to learn for the first time.

  • Revise only your formula sheet, reaction map, and error log : nothing new
  • Pack admit card, documents, and stationery before you sit down to revise
  • Sleep on time ,calculation speed and recall both drop significantly with poor sleep
  • Avoid discussing difficult topics with friends the night before

Sleep is not optional preparation. It is part of preparation.

Exam morning: arrive early, stay calm

Reach the examination center with at least 20–30 minutes to spare. Rushing to the exam on a tight timeline raises cortisol, reduces working memory, and makes the first few questions feel harder than they are. An early arrival gives you time to settle, breathe, and mentally run through your attempt strategy before the paper starts.

Avoid discussing questions or topics with other candidates outside the hall. Someone mentioning a topic you feel uncertain about is enough to shake confidence that took months to build. Stay in your own headspace.

How to use 180 minutes

The biggest time management mistake in CEE is spending too long on difficult questions early and then rushing through easy ones at the end. The two-pass system fixes this.

Time WindowWhat to Do
0–30 minAttempt MAT and your strongest subject. Build rhythm with familiar questions. Do not stall on anything.
30–100 minFirst pass through remaining subjects. Solve every question you can answer confidently and quickly. Mark anything that needs more thought.
100–150 minSecond pass. Return to marked questions. Use elimination. Attempt moderate-difficulty items you skipped.
150–170 minDifficult questions only. Attempt the ones where you can eliminate at least two options. Skip true blind guesses.
170–180 minFinal check. Verify every answer is marked. Look for obvious errors in questions you answered quickly.

The two-pass rule in practice: On your first pass, do not spend more than 30–40 seconds on any single question. If an answer does not come within that time, mark the question and move forward. You will return to it. This protects the easy marks that come later in the paper.

Negative marking ,the rule I wish I had followed earlier

CEE deducts 0.25 marks for every wrong answer. This means four wrong answers cancel one correct answer. Random guessing is mathematically harmful unless you can eliminate options.

I use a simple three-level rule for uncertain questions:

  • Fully confident: Attempt immediately on first pass.
  • Can eliminate 2 options: Attempt on second pass after elimination. The remaining two options give you a 50% chance acceptable odds.
  • No idea / cannot eliminate: Leave it. A blank costs nothing. A wrong answer costs 0.25.

The hardest part of this rule is following it under time pressure. When the clock is running down, the urge to fill every blank is strong. Resist it. A controlled paper with some blanks usually scores higher than a fully attempted paper with random guesses.

Mistakes that cost prepared students marks

MistakeWhat to Do Instead
Spending 3–4 minutes on one difficult question in the first passMark it and move. Return with a fresh perspective in the second pass.
Changing answers without a clear reasonChange an answer only when you find a specific logical error in your first choice.
Skipping the final 10-minute reviewReserve the last 10 minutes specifically for checking ,do not use this time to attempt new questions.
Getting rattled when a section feels hardMove to your stronger subject immediately. Recover confidence, then return.
Attempting blind guesses in the final minutesOnly attempt questions where elimination is possible. Leave the rest blank.

The panic guess trap: In the final 5 minutes, it feels like filling every blank will help. In reality, guessing randomly on 10 uncertain questions is likely to cost you 1–2 marks net, not gain them. Accuracy beats attempt count in a negatively marked exam.

If one section goes badly ,recovery

Sometimes a section feels harder than expected. Maybe Biology questions are more terminology-heavy than your mock tests, or a Physics numerical is taking longer than it should. The wrong response is to keep pushing through that section until the time is gone.

The right response is to move. Switch to a subject where you are more confident, score those marks, and return to the difficult section later. Momentum and confidence affect recall, a few easy questions answered correctly can genuinely make the previously difficult questions feel more manageable when you return.

Final 10 minutes

This time is for checking, not for new attempts. Go through every marked question. Verify that your selected answers are actually marked not just decided mentally. Check for questions where you may have read too quickly in the first pass. Do not introduce new guesses at this stage unless you have a clear elimination reason.

A calm, structured final 10 minutes regularly adds 2–3 marks compared to a panicked last-minute rush. Those 2–3 marks can move your rank meaningfully in a competitive exam.
