CEE Preparation

How to Prepare for CEE Effectively

By Ramu Pandey Published May 2026 8 min read

This guide lays out a practical plan to prepare for the Common Entrance Examination in Nepal. It covers a realistic study schedule, subject prioritisation by weightage, the importance of MCQ practice, and a checklist you can follow to track your progress week by week.

Study schedule and planning

Start with a clear timeline. A typical effective preparation plan spans eight to twelve weeks, split into three phases: foundation, consolidation, and simulation. In the foundation phase you rebuild or reinforce core theory. In consolidation you switch to chapter-wise MCQ practice and review. In simulation you do full-length tests under timed conditions. Each week should have defined goals covering concept completion, targeted MCQ sets, and a small mixed timed practice session.

Weekly breakdown

A structured weekly plan helps you stay on track without burning out. Divide your preparation into three blocks and treat each one as a distinct goal rather than a continuation of the previous one.

  • Weeks one to four: finish core chapters and do short chapter quizzes after each topic.
  • Weeks five to eight: increase MCQ volume, do mixed drills, and keep an error analysis log.
  • Weeks nine to twelve: full-length mock tests and focused revision of weak topics only.

Subject-wise priority

Not all subjects carry equal weight in CEE. Biology, often split into Botany and Zoology combined, tends to have the largest share, followed by Chemistry and Physics. MAT is shorter but fast to improve with consistent practice. Prioritise high-weightage chapters first because they give quick score gains, but do not completely ignore lower-weightage chapters since they still contribute to the final total.

Biology

Focus on human physiology, cell biology, genetics, and commonly tested diagrams. Use short summary notes and flashcards to reinforce terminology and biological processes. Many CEE Biology questions test your ability to recall specific terms quickly, so active recall techniques work better than passive re-reading.

Chemistry

Split your time between physical chemistry practice covering equations and numericals, organic reaction patterns, and quick inorganic facts. Reaction maps help reduce rote memorisation and speed up your answers during the exam. Numericals in physical chemistry are often repeated with small variations, so practising a range of them builds reliable speed.

Physics

Begin with mechanics and modern physics topics that appear repeatedly in past papers. Diagram-based questions and unit handling are frequent sources of careless errors, so practise solving problems with clear, structured steps rather than jumping to the answer. Writing out units at every step is a simple habit that prevents many mistakes.

MAT

Practice short daily timed drills for MAT rather than long study sessions. MAT rewards pattern recognition and speed, and moderate daily practice yields large improvements quickly. Even fifteen minutes a day on MAT-style problems adds up noticeably over a few weeks of consistent effort.

Importance of MCQ practice

MCQ practice does more than evaluate you. It helps build pattern recognition, time management, and error analysis skills simultaneously. Make a habit of keeping an error log and label each mistake as a concept gap, a careless error, or a time-pressure mistake. Re-test those specific questions after three days and again after seven days to confirm the correction has actually stuck.

Online practice resource

Use chapter-wise MCQ practice for targeted improvement between study sessions. You can practise at ceemcq.pandeyramu.com.np which hosts thousands of chapter-wise questions across Biology, Chemistry, Physics, and MAT along with full mock tests timed to CEE conditions.

Session pacing and micro-targets

Set micro-targets for each study session rather than sitting down with a vague plan to study for several hours. A reliable format is forty-five to sixty minutes of focused theory or concept work, followed by ten to fifteen minutes of quick revision of the previous session, then thirty to forty-five minutes of MCQs on the topic just covered. Keep weekly timed mini-tests to measure progress and adjust the plan if a subject is falling behind.

Final CEE exam checklist

Use this checklist in the final weeks before the exam to make sure nothing important has been skipped.

  • Make a weekly study plan with fixed time blocks for each subject.
  • Revise high-weightage chapters first, then cover smaller topics you often forget.
  • Keep an error log for wrong MCQs and revisit it before every practice test.
  • Practice timed MCQs every week to improve both speed and accuracy together.
  • Take at least one full CEE mock test each week during the simulation phase.

What consistent preparation actually looks like

The students who perform well in CEE are rarely those who studied the most hours in total. They are the ones who studied consistently, reviewed their errors honestly, and practised enough MCQs to make pattern recognition automatic. Start early, keep the plan simple, and treat each week's mock test result as information rather than a verdict.
